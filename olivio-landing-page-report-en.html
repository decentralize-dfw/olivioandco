Olivio & Co — Landing Page Implementation Report · March 2026
Part One — Current State

Where does the homepage
stand today?

The Olivio & Co homepage functions as a storefront that obscures the brand's real identity rather than letting it speak. The hero is a collection slider — no message, no trust signal, no directional call to action. Scrolling down reveals collection banners, best sellers, and a Mediterranean illustration. A CTA that links to Amazon, a typo in the hero, and an invisible guarantee policy complete the picture.

The design foundation is clean. Product imagery is strong. Collection names are editorial. The brand identity has genuine potential. The problem is that none of this potential is communicated on the page — visitors leave without understanding why Olivio & Co is worth buying from.

Critical Findings

Six things
not working right now.

Issue 01 · Hero Section
The hero shows products instead of the brand
Critical · Fix Immediately

The first thing a visitor sees is one of three collection slider images. The visuals are high quality but carry no message. "ART OF BREAKFAST", "OLIVIO CLASSIC", "CREATIVE DEDITION" are product category names — not brand value propositions. Why should I buy from Olivio & Co? Is it safe, high quality, right for my child? — none of these are answered.

The hero has one job: answer the question "Why is this brand the right choice for me?" Right now it does not answer that question.
  • Brand value proposition (UV400, guarantee, Mediterranean identity) is entirely absent from the hero
  • Slider format fragments attention — each slide delivers a different message, none of them land
  • CTA "Shop Now" is abstract — it does not say what, why, or which child it is for
  • On mobile the slider is even less effective — most users never see slides two and three
Sorun 02 · Trust Signals
13-month guarantee buried at the bottom of product pages
Critical · Fix Immediately

Olivio & Co has a 13-month guarantee and 30-day returns. Babiators's strongest conversion driver is its free replacement guarantee. Roshambo's 60-day returns are the primary trust signal on their homepage. At Olivio & Co this policy appears as small icon strips at the very bottom of product pages. A parent browsing the homepage never sees it.

A strong existing policy has been made invisible. This is a design placement problem — not a policy creation problem.
  • 13-month guarantee — absent from the homepage entirely
  • 9 design awards exist but reduced to a contextless logo strip
  • 5,500+ Instagram followers, zero social proof visible on the homepage
  • Customer reviews and photo testimonials are absent from the homepage
Issue 03 · Technical Errors
Amazon link + typo + search error
Kritik · Fix Today

Three concrete technical errors are currently active on the homepage. In the Sport Sunglasses section, the "Discover" button links to an Amazon product page — traffic, customer data, and revenue generated by the brand's own site are being handed to Amazon. The "CREATIVE DEDITION" typo appears in the name of the brand's most distinctive collection. The search bar throws an error the moment it opens on any page.

Together, these three errors send the message "this brand is careless" to every potential buyer. The typo is particularly unacceptable for a design-conscious audience.
Issue 04 · No Size Guide
5 age categories — zero size guidance
Important · Near-term

Toddler 0–2, Kids 2–4, Junior 3–7, Junior+ 7–10, and Teen & Adult. There is a 3–4 year overlap between Kids and Junior. A parent buying for a 3.5-year-old does not know which category to choose. No size guide, no head circumference table, no explanation of why the age groups are divided this way. This uncertainty is one of the leading causes of pre-checkout abandonment.

Issue 05 · Blog Hidden
40+ articles — a single link buried in the footer
Important · Near-term

The blog genuinely exists — more than 40 published articles. But it is absent from the main navigation. A parent who arrives looking for information cannot find it and leaves. The content is never used as a trust or authority signal. Additionally, every article is generic — none of it is specific to Olivio & Co.

Issue 06 · Brand Identity Too Shallow
Mediterranean narrative is visually present but lacks depth
Important · Strategic

"Inspired by the Mediterranean" exists as a section headline. But why this story matters, how the Mediterranean aesthetic translates into these eyewear designs, who is behind the brand — none of it is told. Competitors are entirely functional. Olivio & Co's greatest opportunity lies in this cultural identity — but right now it remains at slogan level.

Reference Brands

What can be taken
from six brands?

Each brand is a reference for a different element. The goal is not to copy any of them — it is to adapt a specific application to Olivio & Co. Category differences do not matter; the target audience overlaps.

Referans 01 · Hero & Product Discovery
Ace & Tate
aceandtate.com · Eyewear · Amsterdam
What does it do?
A single, clear message in the hero. After scrolling, clicking a colour variant instantly updates the product image. The "Try at home" button is above the fold. Material details are visible on every product card.
Applied to Olivio & Co
Single message hero + age selector. Instant image update on colour click in product cards. Guarantee or free shipping banner above the fold.
Reference 02 · Editorial Language & Collections
Tinycottons
tinycottons.com · Children's Clothing · Barcelona
What does it do?
Collection pages open like a story. The question "why does this collection have this name, what does it say?" is answered through text and image together. Products appear — embedded within the narrative.
Applied to Olivio & Co
Each of the Breakfast Art, Coral Reef, and Creative Edition D collections should open with a short atmospheric paragraph. The question "where did this collection find its inspiration?" must be answered.
Reference 03 · Artistic Identity & Target Audience
Bobo Choses
bobochoses.com · Children's Clothing · Barcelona
What does it do?
Headline typography, colour use, and collection narratives resonate perfectly with the target audience. It speaks from the perspective of "art for families" rather than "aesthetics for parents". Every collection is presented with an emotional frame.
Applied to Olivio & Co
Position the brand voice as "a brand that brings Mediterranean aesthetics into family life" rather than "a company that sells sunglasses". Rewrite homepage copy within this frame.
Reference 04 · Experience Depth
Wildling Shoes
wildling.de · Parent-Child Footwear · Germany
What does it do?
A small brand whose page experience outperforms much larger ones. Micro-animations, scroll behaviour, and a simple but deep content architecture stand out. The value proposition is stated with crystal clarity in the hero.
Applied to Olivio & Co
Hero scroll behaviour: headline remains fixed while images change beneath it. Short information tooltips on product hover. Smooth transitions between pages.
Reference 05 · Value Narrative & Trust
Cuyana
cuyana.com · Women's Accessories · San Francisco
What does it do?
The "Fewer, better things" manifesto is at the core of the homepage. Navigation is crystal clear. On every product page, material story + place of production + customer review are stacked together. Trust signals appear at every scroll point.
Applied to Olivio & Co
Single-line manifesto below the hero: "Inspired by the Mediterranean, designed for children — every age, 100% UV400." Move the guarantee to above the fold. Consolidate material + guarantee + returns into a single block on product pages.
Reference 06 · Age / Size Segmentation
Allbirds
allbirds.com · Footwear · San Francisco
What does it do?
Navigating between multiple segments (men/women/kids) is seamless. The size selector and material background sit in the same view on product pages. The question "which size should I choose?" is answered by a size guide link directly below the hero.
Applied to Olivio & Co
Move the age category selector below the hero: "Child's age? → Go to the right collection" flow. Add a size guide link to every product card. Resolve the Kids/Junior overlap through the guide.

Section Application — 01

The hero section:
bringing the message to the surface.

The hero currently behaves like a collection showcase window. A single change — replacing the slider with a fixed, message-led hero — can transform the first impression entirely.

Right Now — Problem
Slider format rotates three collections. Each slide has a different image, a different headline (collection name), and a "Shop Now" CTA. Why is this brand trustworthy, why buy from it — neither is addressed. The visitor is not guided, no message lands.
What It Should Be — Solution
Fixed hero, layered message: A large lifestyle image (family, sunlight, Mediterranean atmosphere). Short headline over it: "Inspired by the Mediterranean. Designed for every age." Subline: "UV400 · 13-Month Guarantee · 30-Day Returns" Two CTAs side by side: "Explore Collections" + "Shop by Age"
01
Hero — Reference: Cuyana + Wildling

Remove the slider. Move to a manifesto hero.

The slider format is the weakest hero option for conversion — research consistently shows that 80% of visitors never see slides two and three. A single strong image with a clear message does far more work than three slides. Adapt Cuyana's "Fewer, better things" hero and Wildling's scroll-sticky headline effect to this section.

  • Headline — "Inspired by the Mediterranean, designed for children." One sentence carrying brand identity and value proposition together
  • Trust strip — immediately below the hero headline: "UV400 Protection · 13-Month Guarantee · Free Shipping €69+" — single line, light font
  • Dual CTA — "Explore Collections" (primary) + "Shop by Age" (secondary) — second button anchors to the age selector
  • Image selection — experience, not product: a sunlit beach, a family wearing the glasses, colour and light in the foreground
  • Mobile — the message must be fully readable even in portrait format; CTAs sized for thumb reach
02
Below Hero — Reference: Allbirds + Ace & Tate

Place an age selector directly below the hero.

Combine Allbirds' gender selector with Ace & Tate's categorisation logic. The moment a visitor exits the hero they see: "Who are you buying for?" — and five age categories are displayed with clear icons. Clicking takes them directly to the relevant collection. This resolves the most common pre-purchase question (which category is right for me) immediately below the hero.

  • Format — horizontal icon tab: "0–2y · 2–4y · 3–7y · 7–10y · Teen+" each one clickable
  • Hover behaviour — on hover, show age range + small thumbnail of an example product
  • Linking — each tab goes directly to the relevant collection page; add a tooltip for the Kids/Junior overlap: "Between 3–4? Check our size guide"

Section Application — 02

Trust signals:
making the visible is enough.

The guarantee exists. The awards exist. Moving them above the fold requires no new creation — only a design decision.

Right Now — Problem
The guarantee is small icons at the bottom of product pages. 9 awards are a contextless logo strip mid-page. Customer reviews are nowhere. A parent browsing the homepage sees not a single trust signal.
What It Should Be — Solution
Three-layer trust architecture: (1) Thin strip below hero — guarantee + shipping + returns. (2) Customer review section before "Best Sellers". (3) Awards section with contextual text — "Red Dot Design Award 2024, Germany."
03
Trust Strip — Reference: Cuyana + Babiators

Write the guarantee below the hero in a single line.

Babiators' most effective conversion tool: "Lost or broken, we'll replace it — no questions asked." This sits at the very top of their page. Olivio & Co has a 13-month guarantee but nobody knows about it. Take Cuyana's information strip approach: a single line below the hero with small icons and three pieces of information side by side.

  • Format — thin horizontal strip, full page width: "✓ UV400 Protection  |  ✓ 13-Month Guarantee  |  ✓ Free Returns within 30 Days  |  ✓ Free Shipping €69+"
  • Style — dark background (in brand colour), white text, small icons — distinctive but restrained
  • Link — "13-Month Guarantee" is clickable, linking to the warranty policy page
  • Mobile — switch to 2×2 grid format so all information remains visible
04
Awards Section — Reference: Wildling

Present 9 awards with context — remove the logo strip.

Wildling places its awards on the page with contextual text. Olivio & Co has 9 awards but only the logo. A parent does not recognise the Red Dot logo. "Red Dot Design Award — Germany's most prestigious design honour. Won in 2023 with the Creative Edition D collection." — that sentence transforms a logo into a trust signal.

  • Format change — remove the horizontal scroll strip; create a 3-column grid with logo + name + year + one sentence for each award
  • Lead with 3 awards — surface the most recognisable or most recent three, hide the rest behind a "View All Awards" link
  • Position — between Best Sellers and Collections — credibility peaks, then the product appears
05
Social Proof — Reference: Tinycottons

Move real family photos and reviews onto the homepage.

Tinycottons integrates family photos drawn from Instagram into the page under "As Seen On Real Families". For Olivio & Co, which has 5,500 Instagram followers, this content already exists. After the Judge.me installation, customer reviews can appear in a "What Families Are Saying" section on the homepage.

  • UGC carousel — curated real family photos from Instagram, in a clean grid format
  • Review section — 3–4 curated reviews, each with name + child's age + photo
  • Position — immediately below Best Sellers — after seeing the product, others' experience provides the confirmation

Section Application — 03

Collections and products:
they do not sell without a story.

06
Collections Section — Reference: Tinycottons + Bobo Choses

Add atmospheric text to each collection — visual first, story after.

Tinycottons opens each collection with an atmospheric story. Bobo Choses builds an emotional frame for every collection. Olivio & Co currently reads: "BREAKFAST ART — Eyewear created for kids & family". This text describes nothing about the collection. "A Mediterranean table in the stillness of morning — soft light, coloured vessels, the quiet excitement of a day beginning. The Breakfast Art collection carries that moment." — that sentence sells.

  • Breakfast Art — colours of a Mediterranean morning: "Inspired by morning light, the echo of a moment through a child's eyes."
  • Creative Edition D — lead with the Matisse reference: "Born from Matisse's colour blocks — wearable art pieces for every age."
  • Coral Reef — underwater colour palette: "Coral hues, oceanic transparency — from the depths of the Mediterranean to eyewear."
  • Text length — 1–2 sentences is sufficient; positioned over or directly below the collection image
  • "Creative Dedition" typo — correct to "Creative Edition D" across all pages
07
Best Sellers Carousel — Reference: Ace & Tate

Add colour clicking, context, and micro-information to product cards.

Ace & Tate product cards update their image instantly when a colour is clicked or changed. Hovering reveals material information. At Olivio & Co, product cards are static — colour dots exist but clicking them only opens a different product page. A simple interaction on the card itself significantly increases conversion rate.

  • Colour clicking — clicking a colour dot updates the card image without leaving the page
  • Hover information — "UV400 · Polarised · BPA-Free" as a small badge over the card
  • Age label — a small age tag on each card: "Junior 3–7y" — the buyer sees at a glance which group this is for
  • "Why popular?" — small social proof on the top 3 products: "240 orders this month"
08
Sport & Ski — Reference: Allbirds

Reclaim the sport section for the brand. Remove the Amazon link.

Allbirds' outdoor product page presents environmental context (which activity, which weather conditions) alongside the product. At Olivio & Co, the Sport Sunglasses section routes entirely outside the brand (Amazon). The CTA for this section should go to /collections/sport-glasses; the content should connect "running, hiking, beach, skiing" activities to specific products.

  • Remove Amazon CTA — redirect "Discover" button to /collections/sport-glasses
  • Activity context — match "Running · Hiking · Beach · Skiing" labels to corresponding products
  • Ski Goggles as a separate block — marked with season (October–February), with the message "Ski goggles for children — uncontested territory"

Section Application — 04

Brand identity:
from slogan to narrative.

09
Mediterranean Section — Reference: Bobo Choses + Tinycottons

Deepen the "Inspired by the Mediterranean" section.

The page currently shows a full-screen Mediterranean image with "Eyewear created for kids & family" beneath it. Bobo Choses and Tinycottons transform these sections into brand manifestos — the image stays strong, the text adds meaning. "Where the sun rises earliest, where colours appear most vivid, where children run most freely — these glasses come from there." One paragraph can carry the entire character of a brand.

  • Manifesto paragraph — 2–3 sentences that make the Mediterranean identity concrete; not abstract, but sensory and visual
  • Founder story link — "Meet the Brand" button links to About Us; the About Us page is deepened at the same time
  • Image update — a lifestyle photo (family + product + Mediterranean setting) converts better than landscape alone; if available, use it
10
Blog Activation — Reference: Roshambo + Cuyana

Make the blog visible. Surface content examples on the homepage.

Roshambo's blog generates organic traffic because it is linked from navigation, the homepage, and product pages. Cuyana integrates content blocks into the homepage to reinforce brand authority. At Olivio & Co the blog is hidden in the footer and its content is not brand-specific. Two changes are needed: add the blog to navigation, and surface 2–3 featured articles on the homepage.

  • Navigation — Add a "Guides" or "Blog" tab to the main menu
  • Homepage block — before the footer, a "Need to Know" section with 3 article cards: "Why Children Need Sunglasses · Do Screen Glasses Really Work · How to Choose Ski Goggles"
  • Content prioritisation — Deprioritise generic articles; surface new brand-specific content first
  • Blog → Product linking — Each article links to the relevant collection or product
11
Navigation Improvement — Reference: Ace & Tate

Simplify navigation. Strengthen directional signposting.

Ace & Tate's navigation is crystal clear: product type + collection + lifestyle segments. Olivio & Co's navigation depth is good but trust-building content like the blog, guides, and about us pages are hidden. The top-level menu currently has 5 categories + 2 links. "Guides" or "About Us" should gain first-level visibility.

  • Add to first level — "Guides" or "Blog" and "About Us" — these two links build trust
  • Mega menu improvement — display age groups with visual icons in the "Shop by Age" dropdown
  • Search bar — fix the error; and make the search bar more accessible on every page
  • Top information strip — the "Free Shipping $59+" strip is present and effective; add guarantee information to it as well

Section Application — 05

Conversion opportunities:
preventing abandonment.

12
Size Guide — Reference: Allbirds + Roshambo

Publish the market's most comprehensive children's eyewear size guide.

Allbirds integrates the size selector into the product and a "Can't find your size?" link is visible on every product page. Roshambo's size guide includes visual content and video. Olivio & Co's 6 categories are the perfect framework for this guide — no one else in the market has a system this granular. This guide serves simultaneously as a conversion tool and as the owner of the "children's sunglasses size guide" SEO cluster.

  • Page — publish as /pages/size-guide
  • Table contents — Toddler 0–2 · Kids 2–4 · Junior 3–7 · Junior+ 7–10 · Teen & Adult; each row: age range + head circumference cm + head circumference inches + example product image
  • Kids/Junior overlap — give a clear answer to "which one for 3–4 year olds?": head circumference 48–50cm → Kids; 50cm+ → Junior
  • Access points — "Size Guide" link next to the size selector on every product page · Direct link from the homepage age selector · Listed under Guides in navigation
  • SEO value — long-tail keywords like "sunglasses for age 3", "what size sunglasses 4 year old" are captured by this page
13
Product Page Improvement — Reference: Cuyana + Ace & Tate

Restructure the product page for conversion.

Cuyana's product page stacks material story + place of production + guarantee together. Ace & Tate presents frame details (width, bridge, arm length) interactively. Olivio & Co product pages list UV400 and material information as bullet points — but why it matters, what it means, which child it suits — none of this is answered. Description text stays below 150 words.

  • Description length — minimum 150 words; explain in context why UV400 matters, what polarised lenses do, why BPA-free is preferred
  • Collection story — add 1–2 sentences of collection context to the product description: "This Creative Edition D model was inspired by Matisse's colour block paintings"
  • Guarantee integration — add a "This product is covered by our 13-month guarantee" block at the base of the description — with a clickable link
  • Size guide link — a visible "How do I choose the right size?" link next to the size selector
  • Age fit — age range + head circumference information should also appear within the description text itself
14
Footer & Overall Flow — Reference: Cuyana

Reorganise the footer as a trust centre.

Cuyana's footer presents guarantee, returns, sustainability, and contact information in an organised, trust-affirming way. Olivio & Co's footer contains this information but the hierarchy is flat — everything carries the same weight. The blog link is buried under "ABOUT US" in the footer. The footer is the last thing a visitor sees — trust signals here can tip the purchase decision.

  • Remove blog from footer — move to main navigation; keep only support and policy links in the footer
  • Guarantee emphasis — short guarantee strip at the very top of the footer: "13-Month Guarantee · 30-Day Returns · Free Shipping"
  • Social media connection — social media icons in the footer should be enlarged and accompanied by "Join a community of 5,500+ families"

Summary

The product is exceptional.
The page is not — yet.

Olivio & Co's greatest problem is not the product or the brand — it is that these assets are invisible on the page. A 13-month guarantee exists but is hidden. 9 awards exist but lack context. A Mediterranean identity exists but remains at slogan level. Collection stories exist but are not told.

None of the applications in this report require creating something new. Making existing assets visible, placing them in the right order, and adapting the proven approaches of reference brands to Olivio & Co is sufficient.
