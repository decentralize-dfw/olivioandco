What Is Blue Light?

Blue light is high-energy visible (HEV) light in the 400–500 nanometre range of the electromagnetic spectrum — the shortest visible wavelengths, and therefore the highest energy. It's present in sunlight (where it contributes to mood and alertness), but also emitted intensely by LED screens, fluorescent lighting, and most modern displays.

Blue light zone: 400–500nm — screens emit primarily in this range

Modern LED-backlit screens (phones, tablets, laptops, TVs) emit a higher proportion of blue light than older LCD or CRT displays. This has increased total blue light exposure significantly over the past decade, particularly during evening hours — the period most critical for sleep.

What the Science Actually Says

The honest summary: Blue light glasses are well-evidenced for reducing eye strain and improving sleep quality. They are not proven to prevent permanent eye damage from screens. This distinction matters for how you present them to your child.

The Sleep Mechanism — Why Evening Screen Use Disrupts Sleep

Blue light at ~480nm directly suppresses melatonin production in the pineal gland — the hormone that signals to the body it is time to sleep. In children, melatonin suppression is more pronounced than in adults, and takes longer to recover.

How Blue Light Glasses Work

Lens technology

Blue light filter glasses work by selectively absorbing or reflecting wavelengths in the 400–500nm range. There are two approaches: a physical filter embedded into the lens material during manufacturing, and a coating applied to the lens surface after production. Embedded filters are more durable — they cannot be scratched off and maintain consistent performance over time.

The filtering typically produces a mild warm tint — slightly more yellow than a clear lens. Quality blue light lenses filter 30–50% of blue light; some premium lenses reach 70%+. For regular daytime use, 30–40% filtering provides sufficient benefit without significant colour distortion.

From What Age Are Screen Glasses Relevant?

Screen glasses are most beneficial for school-age children — those who use devices regularly for learning and leisure. For children under 4, the stronger intervention is reducing screen time itself, not filtering it.

Age guidelines

  • Under 2: No screens; screen glasses irrelevant.
  • 2–4 years: Limit to 1h/day; screen glasses have marginal benefit at this usage level.
  • 4–7 years: School devices begin. Eye strain emerges. Screen glasses start to add value.
  • 7–12 years: Strong candidate. Extended daily device use at school and home. Sleep impact real.
  • 12+ years: High benefit. 8h+ screen exposure. Evening use directly impacts sleep and next-day concentration.
